# The-truth
Is Jesus Divine or a Prophet?

Mark 13:32 – Jesus’ Self‑Limited Knowledge
Claim: “Only the Father knows the day and hour” (Mark 13:32) proves Jesus isn’t God.
Rebuttal: In Christian theology, Jesus is both fully God and fully human. The most common explanation is kenosis (Philippians 2:6‑7): when He became man, Jesus voluntarily “emptied” Himself of some divine prerogatives for a time
. In other words, He did not cease being God, but He willingly did not access certain divine knowledge while on earth. As one commentator notes, Jesus “abdicated His rights to manifest the full nature of deity… part of His deity was in sleep mode”
. Thus when Jesus says He does not know “the day or hour,” it reflects His incarnate humility and trust in the Father (cf. John 5:30; 8:28–29), not a denial of His divinity. In fact, Jesus will return (and then “only the Father knows” no longer applies) as the triumphant Son of Man.

“Philippians 2:6‑7 describes Christ ‘taking the form of a bond-servant… being born in the likeness of men.’ He voluntarily limited His divine prerogatives, including His knowledge, to live fully as a human
.”

John 7:16 – “Not My Own” Teaching
Claim: Jesus’ statement “My teaching is not my own” (John 7:16) suggests He isn’t divine.
Rebuttal: Jesus consistently taught that His words came from the Father (John 5:19; 8:28), fulfilling the Father’s will. This does not negate His deity; rather, it highlights the unity of the Trinity. John 7:16 means Jesus is the faithful Agent of God, the “conduit” for divine truth. As one analysis explains, “God [is] the origin of a matter and Jesus is his conduit through whom the matter comes”
. In fact, other parts of John’s Gospel emphasize Jesus’ divine identity (e.g. John 1:1, 14; 10:30; 14:9). Saying His teaching comes from the Father merely underscores that, as the sinless Son, He speaks only what God truly wills.

Scriptural Context: Jesus elsewhere claims oneness with the Father and divine authority. For example, “I and the Father are one” (John 10:30) and “before Abraham was, I am” (John 8:58) clearly identify Him with Yahweh.
Unity of Mission: The Father sending the Son with a message is exactly what the Incarnation is about. 1 Corinthians 8:6 says “for us there is one God…the Father…and one Lord, Jesus Christ, through whom are all things.” In that sense Jesus’ teaching “not [of] himself” is exactly how “all things” come through Him from the Father
.
“Jesus does not teach from Himself but what originates from God who sent Him. God is the origin and Jesus is the conduit
.”

Luke 6:12 – All‑Night Prayer
Claim: Jesus praying all night to the Father (Luke 6:12) implies He is not God.
Rebuttal: Even as God, Jesus prayed regularly during His earthly life (e.g. Mark 1:35; Luke 5:16; John 17). Prayer was part of His human experience and example, and it also expresses the personal relationship within the Trinity. Far from contradicting His deity, the New Testament explicitly affirms Jesus’ divine identity: “Jesus is God and the Son of God. God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit are three distinct Persons with equal glory and the same essence; together, they are one God”
.

Distinction of Persons: The Bible often shows Jesus praying to the Father and teaching equality with the Father. For instance, John 17 records Jesus praying to the Father while elsewhere He declares “I and the Father are one.” This demonstrates distinct persons, not separation of deity.
Trinitarian Explanation: One commentary notes that Jesus “prays to God” (Luke 6:12) as part of His incarnate role, but Jesus remains divine. His prayer life simply shows the Father and Son as distinct persons. The same source concludes: “That Jesus the Son can pray to His Father shows they are distinct Persons; that His holiness makes Him a sufficient sacrifice… shows He is God.”
Human Example: Jesus’ all-night prayer (choosing the Twelve) is recorded nowhere else, because Luke wanted to highlight His dependence on the Father for big decisions. The point is pastoral (we too should seek God in prayer), not a doctrine of His nature. Even human kings consult advisors and guidance; it doesn’t make the king one of them, just diligent and wise.
“Jesus… regularly prays to the Father… Jesus is God and the Son of God… That Jesus the Son can pray to His Father shows they are distinct Persons
.”

John 20:17 – “My Father and Your Father… My God and Your God”
Claim: Jesus calling the Father “my God” (John 20:17) shows He is not God.
Rebuttal: This scene occurs right after the Resurrection, as Mary Magdalene clings to Jesus. He tells her to go to the disciples, saying, “I am ascending to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God.” This reflects His role as the faithful Son, not a contradiction. The Father is Jesus’ God in His human nature. As one analyst notes, a difference in role/function does not mean inferiority in nature: Jesus obeys the Father because of His incarnate mission, not because He is ontologically lesser
.

Trinitarian View: Within the Trinity, the Son can reverence the Father. It’s natural for Jesus, even though He is divine, to address the Father with filial language in His human role. Other persons of the Trinity also address one another (e.g. “the Lord said to my Lord…” in Psalm 110).
Evidence of Deity: Ironically, in the very Gospel of John, Jesus is later acknowledged as God. John 20:28 has Thomas exclaim to the risen Lord, “My Lord and my God!” This is a clear confession of Jesus’ deity, and Jesus does not correct him
. That same passage we’re discussing only strengthens the point: Jesus spoke of doing the will of the Father, yet elsewhere He is worshipped as God.
Analogy: Think of a divine ambassador: he fully represents his king but still calls that king “my lord.” Jesus’ use of “my God” is like an ambassador saying “our king” – it shows loyalty and obedience, not loss of status.
“A difference in function does not mean an inferiority in nature
… This Son is also the eternal Lord that has an assured will now with the Father… as Thomas says in [John 20:28], ‘My Lord and my God!’
.”

Original Language and Dating of Scripture
Aramaic vs. Greek: Jesus primarily spoke Aramaic. The common Aramaic word for God was “Alaha” or “Eloho”
 – linguistically related to Hebrew Elohim. (In fact, the Arabic Allah shares the same Semitic root, but as scholars note, Allah is no more unique than Elohim; they all just mean “God” in related languages
.) When the Gospel writers wrote in Greek, they translated “Alaha” as Theos (God) or used the Septuagint style Kyrios (Lord) for the divine name. This was no trick – Greek was the New Testament’s lingua franca. Translating Jesus’ Aramaic words into Greek did not change their meaning. For example, His cry “Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?” (Aramaic for “My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?”) is faithfully recorded in Greek as “ho theos mou ho theos mou”
.

Translation is expected: Jesus’ life and teachings were transmitted by bilingual witnesses. The core message (Jesus as Messiah/God) comes through clearly in Greek despite the language shift.
Theologically, calling God “Alaha/Allah” in Aramaic vs. Greek Theos is irrelevant; all refer to the same monotheistic God of Israel. The Scriptures themselves use multiple names and titles for God in Hebrew (Elohim, Yahweh, Adonai, etc.) without confusion.
Dating of the Gospels vs. Quran: The Gospels were written in the 1st century AD, not centuries later. For example:

Matthew: Likely written AD 55–65
. The author never mentions Jerusalem’s destruction in 70 AD (a major prophecy of Jesus), implying it was written before that event
.
Mark & Luke: Strongly datable to the 50s–60s AD based on historical clues and early citations (e.g. Luke’s Gospel is quoted as “Scripture” by AD 64 in 1 Timothy
).
John: Written later, around AD 85–90
, which is still well within the first century.
All the New Testament writings were composed within a generation or two of Jesus’ life, often by eyewitnesses or their associates (Paul’s letters start by AD 50). In contrast, the Qur’an was compiled about 600 years later, in the 7th century AD. That Christian Gospels came decades after Christ (not immediately) is well-documented; early church fathers like Polycarp and Papias confirm circulation by AD 100+. Their early dating and the volume of manuscripts (P52 fragment ~AD 125–150) attest to the Gospels’ authenticity.

“The Gospel of Matthew… was likely written between AD 55 and 65, within a generation of Jesus’ death
… If Matthew had written after AD 70 he likely would have mentioned the temple’s destruction
.”

Irrelevance of Demographics
Claim: “Islam is fastest growing, many revert, so Christianity must be false.”
Rebuttal: Sociological facts about religious growth do not determine truth. Truth is not a popularity contest. Countless people have adopted or abandoned various religions throughout history. The validity of Christian claims rests on historical and textual evidence, not census data.

Whether a religion spreads widely says nothing about the correctness of its theology. Christianity itself was once a tiny sect yet made truth-claims about Jesus’ resurrection and deity. Those claims stand or fall on evidence (archaeology, manuscript reliability, fulfilled prophecy, etc.), independent of how many follow.
In debate, appealing to large numbers is a logical fallacy (argumentum ad populum). It’s better to stick to clear scriptural and historical facts, which we have done above.
Conclusion
Each “proof-text” (Mark 13:32; John 7:16; Luke 6:12; John 20:17) can be understood in harmony with Jesus’ divinity when the full biblical teaching is considered. Jesus’ statements about the Father’s knowledge, His source of teaching, and His prayers all fit the New Testament portrayal of the Triune God – one God in three persons – and of Jesus as the incarnate Word. Moreover, the language differences and manuscript dates do not undermine these truths. In short, the passages cited do not “crush” the doctrine of Christ’s deity; they deepen our understanding of how the Son relates to the Father.
